# Debugging the Holiday Hangover: How I’m Making Sense of My December Spending

Is it just me, or does the sparkle of December always seem to leave a little bit of a _hangover_ come January?

I’m sitting here at my desk—the same spot where I spend my days working in tech—staring at my banking app. Usually, I’m focused on code or project deadlines. Today, I’m trying to **debug my own finances**.

If you're feeling that post-holiday heaviness when you look at your credit card balance, take a deep breath. You aren't alone, and you haven't failed. This is just the beginning of your journey in learning financial self-care basics.

I’m right there with you, learning how to navigate this in real time.

## What I Noticed This Year

When I opened my statements this morning, I felt that familiar pit in my stomach. But instead of closing the tab, I decided to just… look.

Here’s what surprised me about my own patterns:

- **The “Convenience Tax”**  
  A lot of my debt didn’t come from big, shiny gifts. It came from $15 delivery fees when I was too tired to cook or last-minute expedited shipping during holiday chaos.

- **The Emotional “Yes”**  
  Saying yes to every festive happy hour felt generous in the moment. Looking back, I can see how much of that spending was tied to wanting to make everyone happy—even when it meant less peace for January me.

- **The Subscription Creep**  
  A few “free trials” for holiday movies that quietly rolled into paid subscriptions I forgot to cancel.

## Understanding the Concept: What Is a “Debt Hangover”?

_Note: This section covers general financial concepts for educational purposes._

In personal finance, a **holiday hangover** usually refers to the gap between holiday spending and the arrival of January’s billing statements.

One helpful concept here is **accrued interest**. When we put purchases on a credit card and don’t pay the balance in full, the cost of those items increases over time because of interest (APR).

If you want to dive deeper into the math, check out our guide on understanding interest rates to see how a $50 sweater can quietly turn into $60 or $70.

For authoritative guidance on how credit card interest works, I often reference the **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)**.

## The Psychology of the “Fresh Start Effect”

Why does January feel so different from December?

Behavioral scientists talk about the **Fresh Start Effect**—the idea that “temporal landmarks” like New Year’s Day help us separate our past selves from our ideal future selves.

The catch? Fresh starts can push us into overly ambitious goals that lead to burnout.

Instead of trying to fix everything at once, I’m using January energy to **observe**, not overhaul. I’m getting curious about _why_ I spent—not just how much.

## Shifting From Guilt to Clarity

I used to think being “good with money” meant never having debt.

Now I’m learning it actually means **understanding my patterns**.

What helped me wasn’t a strict no-spend rule—it was asking one simple question:

_“What was I feeling when I bought this?”_

That shift helped me see debt as data, not a personal failure.

---

## Your 15-Minute “Debt Debugging” Audit

If you’re overwhelmed, skip the 12-month plan. Try this instead:

1. **Subscription Scan**  
   Open your phone’s subscription settings and cancel one thing you didn’t use in December.

2. **High-Interest Highlight**  
   Identify the card with the highest APR. Even small reductions in high-interest debt can make a big difference over time.

3. **Joy Review**  
   Find one December purchase that brought real, lasting joy. Money aligned with values isn’t wasted.
